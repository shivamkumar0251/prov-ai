## Project Scoping & Domain Boundaries

Avoid the **“One Giant Graph”** Trap!

<Tip>
  **Best Practice**: Create separate Prov AI projects per business use case or domain, instead of building one massive, mixed graph for the entire company.
</Tip>

### Why separate projects?

- **Terminology Alignment**: Different domains often have different definitions for the same term (e.g., “Revenue” in Finance vs. “Revenue” in Marketing).
- **Reduced Hallucinations**: A single giant graph with mixed semantics makes it easier for the AI to pick the wrong tables or mix conflicting definitions.
- **Focused Context**: Smaller projects provide cleaner semantic layers, clearer Instructions, and specific Question-SQL Pairs.
- **Easier Governance**: Testing and permissions are simpler to manage at the domain level.

---

## How to Scope Projects

Use separate projects for distinct business areas:

- **Marketing & Growth**: Campaigns, channels, attribution, CAC, ROAS.
- **Product Analytics**: Events, feature usage, funnels, retention.
- **Sales & Revenue**: Pipeline, bookings, MRR, ARR.
- **Operations & Logistics**: Lead times, yield, utilization, defects.

<Note>
  **Rule of Thumb**: If a domain has its own stakeholders and its own unique metric definitions, it almost always deserves its own Prov AI project.
</Note>

## Define Your “First 20 Questions”

Don’t start by modeling everything. Start by modeling what people actually ask.

1.  **Survey Stakeholders**: List 10–20 questions they ask weekly (e.g., daily checks, board reports).
2.  **Identify Core Metrics**: MRR, AOV, LTV, Churn, CAC, etc.
3.  **Categorize Questions**:
    - **Simple Retrieval**: Counts, sums, basic grouping.
    - **Business Metric**: Specific, calculated formulas.
    - **Complex Analysis**: Window functions, multi-table joins.

Use this list to decide what must be clear in **Semantics**, what needs **Instructions**, and what deserves **Question-SQL Pairs**.

## Step 0: Preprocess the Data

Create analytics-friendly reporting tables or views per domain to simplify complex business logic *before* you model semantics.

### Why this matters
- **LLM Reliability**: AI is much more reliable with explicit columns (booleans, fiscal periods) than with complex string parsing.
- **Error Prevention**: Prevents inconsistent definitions (e.g., forgetting to exclude test accounts) across different natural language questions.

### Best Practices for Preprocessing
- **Boolean Flags**: Add fields like `is_partner`, `is_test_account`, or `is_valid_revenue`.
- **Pre-join Dimensions**: Connect fiscal calendars or accounts early. Add `fiscal_quarter` or `fiscal_year` columns.
- **Flatten Data**: Denormalize frequently-used joins into a single "reporting" model to reduce join complexity for the AI.

---

### Examples for Preprocessing

#### Example: “Net Revenue last quarter” (exclude refunds/canceled/test/internal)

**Before (Raw tables, easy to miss rules):**
```sql
SELECT SUM(amount) AS revenue
FROM payments
WHERE paid_at >= '2025-10-01' AND paid_at < '2026-01-01'
  AND status = 'paid';
```

**Common problems with this raw approach:**
- It doesn't exclude refunds or chargebacks.
- It might include canceled orders or internal test accounts.
- “Last quarter” might need fiscal quarter logic, not just calendar dates.

---

**Step 0 Preprocessing (Create a reporting-ready view):**
The best way is to encode flags and fiscal fields once in your data layer:

```sql
CREATE OR REPLACE VIEW finance_revenue_facts AS
SELECT
  p.payment_id,
  p.amount,
  /* Business rules as booleans */
  (p.status = 'paid') AS is_paid,
  (p.refund_amount IS NOT NULL AND p.refund_amount > 0) AS is_refunded,
  (c.is_test_account = TRUE) AS is_test,
  /* Net amount calculation */
  (p.amount - COALESCE(p.refund_amount, 0)) AS net_amount,
  fc.fiscal_year_quarter
FROM payments p
JOIN customers c ON p.customer_id = c.customer_id
JOIN fiscal_calendar fc ON DATE(p.paid_at) = fc.calendar_date;
```

**After (Prov AI queries become simple + consistent):**
```sql
SELECT SUM(net_amount) AS net_revenue
FROM finance_revenue_facts
WHERE fiscal_year_quarter = '2025Q4'
  AND is_paid = TRUE
  AND is_refunded = FALSE
  AND is_test = FALSE;
```

**Why this is better:**
- The “Net revenue” logic is encoded once (not re-invented per question).
- Fiscal quarters are always correct.
- Exclusions are harder to forget, leading to zero "looks right but is wrong" mistakes.

## Step 1: Speak Your Business Language

The semantic layer is where you tell Prov AI what the data represents: entities, metrics, and relationships.

### 1.1 Business-First Descriptions
Describe tables as real-world objects, not technical storage:
- **Avoid**: `daily_report` – "production table".
- **Use**: "Daily actual production status per machine" – helps the AI distinguish between "Goals" and "Actuals".

### 1.2 Mark Measures and Dimensions
- **Measures/Metrics**: Numeric fields for summing/averaging. In descriptions, use "Total X" or "Amount of Y".
- **Dimensions**: Dates, categories, and IDs used for filtering. Explicitly mention "Date for..." or "Category of...".
- **Entity Identifiers**: Mark IDs as entity keys (e.g., "Unique identifier for customer"). This helps the AI infer `COUNT(DISTINCT customer_id)` when asked for "number of customers".

### 1.3 Define Relationships
Explicitly declare **one-to-one** or **one-to-many** relationships in the UI. Don't rely on the LLM to "discover" joins at the SQL level.

---

## Step 2: Set Universal Rules with Instructions

Instructions are reusable rules that shape SQL generation and chart behavior.

### 2.1 Global Instructions
Configure these for every project:
- **Exclusion Rules**: "Exclude orders with status 'canceled' from revenue calculations."
- **Date Defaults**: "When a question mentions 'orders last week', default to the `order_created_at` field."
- **Formatting**: "Round monetary metrics to 2 decimal places" or "Format rates as percentages."

### 2.2 Question-Matching Instructions
Use these for tricky domain concepts like "late delivery" or "churned customer."

## Step 4: Human-in-the-Loop Iteration

Treat Prov AI as a living system. When a user asks a question:
- If correct → **Save to Knowledge**.
- If almost right but missing a filter → **Add an Instruction**.
- If wrong → **Refine the Semantics**.
